## In public spaces

- Avoid eating or drinking while walking; stop somewhere suitable instead.
- Keep your voice low and your belongings compact on public transport.
- Follow local signs and queues, even when the reason is not obvious.

I found that the easiest approach was to **pause and observe**. Where are people waiting? Is a space quiet? Are shoes being removed? You do not need to know every custom before arriving if you are willing to notice what is happening around you.

## In restaurants

**Do not leave a tip.** It is not part of normal service culture, except in a few specific situations such as some private-guide services.

I also avoid treating unfamiliar service as a problem to correct. A restaurant may have its own way of ordering, seating guests or returning trays. Following the signs and the staff’s gestures is usually enough.

## A little Japanese goes a long way

In my experience, people were often kind when I looked lost, especially when I tried **a few words of Japanese**. Outside major cities, you may also attract a little curiosity. Stay patient and respectful.

The goal is not perfect pronunciation. A greeting, _sumimasen_ and a sincere thank you can make an awkward moment feel much warmer.
