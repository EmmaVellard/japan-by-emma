## Look up—and downstairs

Restaurants are often **on upper floors or in basements**. The street-level shopfront is only part of what is available, so check building directories and map results nearby.

This changed the way I walked through Japanese cities. A building that appears quiet from the pavement can contain several completely different places to eat.

## How I read reviews

I do not rely on one score. I read recent comments and look at what people actually ordered before deciding whether a restaurant suits me.

My broader Google map contains places I am still researching. Mapstr is different: it is where I keep notes on restaurants and hotels I have actually tried.

## Queues are normal

**Queues are normal.** Popular restaurants often have lines, and not every place takes reservations. Keep one nearby alternative and avoid building an entire evening around a single unbookable restaurant.

I try to decide how long I am genuinely happy to wait before joining the queue. Leaving is not a failed plan; it may be how you discover the place next door.

## Konbini backup

Convenience stores are useful when it is late or you need something easy. Bentos and other prepared food can often be heated in the shop, which makes them a reassuring fallback after a delayed train or a long travel day.

Some of the best travel memories arrive around a carefully chosen meal. Others are a warm konbini dinner eaten quietly after a day that became longer than expected. I leave room for both.
