## How I would organise the day

I would arrive through **Katase-Enoshima or Enoshima Station**, cross to the island and follow the main route gradually. The approach passes the lively Benzaiten Nakamise street before climbing towards Enoshima Shrine, the Samuel Cocking Garden and the Sea Candle.

If the weather and opening conditions allow it, continue towards the quieter far side of the island and the Iwaya Caves. I would keep enough time for the return walk instead of treating the caves as one last hurried stop.

## A pace that feels right

- Let the island occupy most of the day rather than combining every part of Kamakura and Enoshima.
- Expect stairs and slopes, even if using the outdoor escalators for part of the climb.
- Leave room for changing coastal weather and long views when the sky is clear.
- Finish near the water if the light and energy make it worthwhile.

## Food and small pauses

The main approach is busy with snacks, cafés and seafood restaurants. I would choose one proper pause rather than eating while trying to keep moving uphill. On crowded days, walking a little farther can make the meal feel calmer.

## Transport

Enoshima is roughly an hour from central Tokyo. The Enoden also connects Fujisawa and Kamakura, making the train journey part of the coastal experience. For a combined Kamakura–Enoshima day, decide which place matters most before fixing the route.

Opening conditions for the caves and coastal facilities can change with weather or safety conditions. Check the official information on the day of the visit.
